## 1. Chart Selection Guide ⭐ (the heart of this course)

| Chart                 | Best for                                                                                                    | Example / notes                                                           |
| --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Line**              | **Trends over a continuous variable** (usually time), equal intervals, multiple series                      | Product sales changing over time                                          |
| **Pie**               | Breakdown of a whole into proportions — parts sum to **100%**                                               | Marketing budget split by channel. One data series only, ≤ ~12 categories |
| **Bar** (horizontal)  | Comparing related datasets/categories — categories on the **vertical** axis                                 | Population of 10 countries                                                |
| **Stacked bar**       | Category comparison + composition — sub-bars stacked end-to-end                                             | Country population split into age ranges                                  |
| **Column** (vertical) | Side-by-side comparison; discrete change over time; handles **negative + positive values** well             | Page views vs. session time month-to-month                                |
| **Area**              | Like a line chart but fills below the line to **emphasize magnitude over time**                             | Cumulative revenue                                                        |
| **Treemap**           | Complex **hierarchies** via nested rectangles — uses **color, size, and closeness**                         | State employment: size = population, color = rate                         |
| **Funnel**            | Pipeline / stages of a continuous process                                                                   | Conversion rate from lead → sale                                          |
| **Scatter**           | **Correlations, trends, clusters, patterns** between two numeric variables; color = category, size = volume | Units sold vs. revenue per product line                                   |
| **Bubble**            | Scatter variant for comparing a **handful of categories**                                                   | Areas of significant expenditure in sales budgets                         |
| **Histogram**         | **Distribution** of data grouped into bins — NOT a bar chart (bars compare, histograms distribute)          | Order value distribution                                                  |
| **Filled map**        | Data containing **geographical regions**                                                                    | Sales by province                                                         |
| **Sparkline**         | Tiny in-cell trend, **no axis**                                                                             | Intraday stock movement                                                   |
| **Waterfall**         | **Financial data** — running effect of sequential +/− values                                                | Profit bridge                                                             |

💡 The one-line decision rule (graded quiz Q9): **comparison across categories → bar/column; change over time → line/time series; share of a whole → pie; relationship between two numbers → scatter.**

### Audience-centered choice — my own example (from the AI dialogue)
Why visuals work: engagement + memorability + **pre-attentive attributes**, showing relationships between parts, **reducing cognitive load**, directing spatial hierarchy.

> [!tip] My pie-chart lesson (keep this for interviews)
> In my freelance sales systems, showing *total paid vs. money owed* as a **pie chart** would be a bad choice: percentages of a whole give the client nothing actionable. What they need is **direct comparison and cash flow over time** — a **column chart** (ideally over time) delivers both. Chart choice = what the audience must *act on*, not what looks nice.

## 2. Charting in Excel

### Basic charts
Insert from selected data: line, pie, bar. Line = data in columns/rows, multiple series OK. Pie = single series, few categories.

### PivotCharts ⭐
- A **PivotChart uses data summarized in a PivotTable** — that's the entire difference from a standard chart
- Filtering a PivotChart **automatically updates the connected PivotTable** (and vice versa) — they share state
- Expansion state too: to show BMW expanded while others stay collapsed, **expand BMW in the connected PivotTable** — the chart mirrors it
- Great for area and column charts on summarized data

### Advanced charts
- **Treemap** — proportions as rectangular shapes (hierarchies)
- **Scatter** — statistical, scientific, and engineering comparisons
- **Histogram** — bin-based distribution (Axis Options controls bin size)
- **Filled map** — geographic regions
- **Sparklines** — set location by selecting a cell (auto-fills Location Range) or typing the cell reference into the Location Range box
- **Waterfall** — financial data

## 3. Dashboards

**Why dashboards:** insights at a glance · alert to trends/patterns · **interactive via filters** · **dynamically update as data changes** · centralized consolidated view.

**Golden rule:** a dashboard shows the **most important/relevant information in a single place** — *need to know*, not *nice to know*. **Too much information means your key points get lost** (SME advice; also quiz answer: more visuals ≠ better → False).

**Tool tiers:**
- Basic spreadsheet apps: Excel, Google Sheets
- Advanced analysis & viz apps: **Tableau, IBM Cognos Analytics, Bokeh/Dash (Python), R Shiny** (full-desktop Excel and Google Sheets do NOT count as "advanced" — quiz trap)
- My market note: **Power BI** belongs in that advanced tier too and is the Ottawa/GC priority — this course's dashboard concepts (KPI cards, filters, drill-down, layout discipline) transfer directly to my PL-300 prep.

### Pre-dashboard checklist (Excel)
- [ ] Collect and organize the data
- [ ] Verify clean & error-free, no blank rows/columns
- [ ] **Format the data as a table**
- [ ] Create pivot tables for the analysis layer
- [ ] Build the visualizations to populate the dashboard (charts, maps, slicers)
- Control what a chart displays precisely with **Filters**

## 6. Quick Self-Check (quiz answer bank)

### Charts
- Bar vs column → bar puts categories on the **vertical** axis, column on the horizontal
- Nested rectangles → **Treemap**; treemaps represent hierarchy via **color, size, closeness**
- Value over months/quarters → **Line chart**; line charts show **trends/changes in related data over time**
- Pie slices together = **100% of the whole**; best for share-of-total (e.g., each car model's share of unit sales)
- Compare values across categories → **Bar chart**
- Analyze + present a data story → **PowerPoint and Excel**
- Area vs column → area **fills beneath a line to emphasize magnitude over time**; column compares categories
- Histogram vs bar → **bars compare, histograms show distribution** (bins)
- Filled maps → **geographical region data**; sparklines → **trends** (no axis)
- Scatter → **statistical, scientific, engineering** comparisons; waterfall → **financial data**
- Sparkline placement → select a cell (auto Location Range) or type the reference
- Storytelling: visuals help analysts **choose what to emphasize** and help stakeholders **see patterns faster** — they're not decoration and not only for technical audiences

### PivotCharts
- PivotChart = chart built on **PivotTable-summarized data**
- Filter the chart → the **PivotTable updates automatically**
- Expansion state lives in the **PivotTable**; the chart reflects it

### Dashboards
- Purpose → **most important/relevant info in a single place**
- Benefits → dynamic updates, trend/pattern alerts, insights (NOT app performance monitoring)
- Max info = better → **False**; too much info loses key points
- Advanced viz apps → **Tableau, Cognos, Bokeh** (not desktop Excel / Google Sheets)
- Before building → **clean data, format as table, remove blank rows, create pivot tables, create visualizations**
- Precise control of displayed info → **Filters**

## 🎯 Interview Questions — JDA Path (visualization & dashboards)

Visualization questions test judgment, not tool clicks. Anchor answers in the audience and the decision they need to make.

**Q1. How do you choose the right chart for a dataset?**
> I start from the relationship in the data and the decision the audience faces: comparison across categories → bar or column; change over time → line; share of a whole → pie (single series, few categories); correlation between two numeric variables → scatter; hierarchy → treemap; process stages → funnel; distribution → histogram. Then I sanity-check against the audience — the technically "valid" chart can still be the wrong one if it doesn't support the action they need to take.

**Q2. Give an example of a chart choice that would mislead or fail an audience.**
> From my own freelance sales systems: presenting *total paid vs. money owed* as a pie chart. It's technically accurate — two parts of a whole — but percentages give a client nothing to act on. What they need is direct comparison of actual amounts and how cash flow moves over time, which a column chart over time delivers. Chart choice is about actionability, not correctness.

**Q3. What's the difference between a bar chart and a histogram?**
> They look alike but answer different questions. A bar chart **compares** discrete categories; a histogram shows the **distribution** of one continuous variable grouped into bins. Sales by region → bar. How order values spread from $10 to $500 → histogram.

**Q4. What is a PivotChart and why use one over a regular chart?**
> A PivotChart is built on data summarized by a PivotTable, and the two stay synchronized — filter or expand one and the other updates. That makes it ideal for interactive exploration of large datasets: I can drill from year → quarter, or expand one manufacturer while others stay collapsed, without rebuilding anything.

**Q5. What makes a good dashboard?**
> Ruthless prioritization: need-to-know, not nice-to-know — the most important information in a single place. Too many visuals bury the key points. Beyond that: interactivity through filters/slicers so users answer their own follow-ups, dynamic connection to source data so it stays current, and a layout that puts headline KPIs first with supporting detail below.

**Q6. Walk me through how you'd build a dashboard in Excel.**
> Foundation first: clean the data (no blanks, no duplicates), format it as a table, then build pivot tables as the analysis layer. Only then do I create the visualizations — pivot charts, maps, KPI cells — and assemble them on a dashboard sheet with slicers wired to multiple pivots so one click filters everything. The dashboard is the last 20%; data preparation is the real work.

**Q7. What's the difference between a filter, a slicer, and a timeline?**
> All three restrict what's shown. Filters are the underlying mechanism, precise but hidden in menus. Slicers are on-screen buttons — the user-friendly face of filtering, ideal on dashboards for non-technical users. Timelines are slicers specialized for dates, letting users drag across months or quarters.

**Q8. You have hierarchical data — sales by country → province → city. How do you visualize it?**
> A treemap: nested rectangles where size carries one measure and color another, so two dimensions read at a glance. For interactive use I'd add drill-down — in Cognos via navigation paths, in Looker Studio via stacked dimensions with the drill toggle, in a PivotChart via expand/collapse.

**Q9. Have you worked with any BI tools?**
> Yes — IBM Cognos Analytics and Google Looker Studio through my IBM Data Analyst certification: templated dashboards, calculated fields (e.g., Margin = unit price − unit cost), navigation paths and drill-downs in Cognos; multi-page reports, SQL-style data blending, and viewer-side controls in Looker. The concepts map directly onto Power BI, which I'm building depth in now for the PL-300 — and my decade of SQL and full-stack work means the data-modeling side of BI tools comes naturally.

**Q10. In Looker Studio, how would you combine data from two tables into one chart?**
> Blend Data — Looker's join feature. Upload both sources, open the chart's Blend Data panel, set each table's dimensions and metrics, then configure the join (typically a left outer join on the shared key, like Cust_ID). Conceptually identical to a SQL LEFT JOIN, which made it immediately familiar.

**Q11. How do you tell a story with data?**
> Structure the presentation around the audience's decision, in their business language. Lead with the takeaway, support it with the one or two visuals that carry the message, and share sources, assumptions, and validations to earn trust. A presentation is not a data dump — everything that doesn't serve the business question gets cut.

**Q12. A stakeholder asks for "all the numbers" on one dashboard. How do you respond?**
> I'd dig into the underlying need: which decisions do they make with this, and how often? Then propose a layered design — headline KPIs on the main view, with drill-downs, filters, or secondary pages for the detail. They keep access to everything, but the key points stay visible. Displaying everything at once means the important signals get lost — the opposite of what they actually want.

**Q13. When would you use a scatter chart vs. a bubble chart?**
> Scatter for the relationship between two numeric variables across many points — spotting correlation, clusters, outliers. Bubble when comparing a handful of categories and I need a third measure encoded in bubble size (and often a fourth in color) — like product lines plotted by quantity sold vs. revenue with size showing volume.

**Q14. What KPIs would you put on a sales dashboard?** *(mirrors my final project)*
> Depends on the manager's decisions, but for a dealer-network manager I built exactly this: total profit (formatted in $M), total and average quantity sold, quantity by model, and profit by dealer sorted so under-performers surface immediately — plus a service view with recalls per model, customer sentiment, and a monthly sold-vs-profit combo chart to expose margin pressure.

**Q15. Tell me about a dashboard you built — and what it revealed.** *(⭐ the SwiftAuto story with real numbers)*
> For my IBM certification capstone I built a two-page dashboard for a car dealership chain analyzing sales and profit per dealer. The Sales page carried the headline KPIs — total profit in $M, quantity sold, average per dealer — with profit by dealer sorted ascending so weak performers surfaced first. The Service page correlated recalls per model, customer sentiment, and a monthly sold-vs-profit combo chart. The analysis surfaced concrete insights: one dealer (ID 1288) led the network at 2,644 units and also earned the highest profit on the Hudson model, while the Champlain model had the lowest total profit across all dealers — exactly the kind of finding that drives inventory and marketing decisions. Both dashboard pages scored perfect marks, partly because I applied a QA rule from an earlier project: verify every exported deliverable page by page before it ships.

**Q16. Your dashboard scored perfectly but your workbook lost points. What did you take from that?**
> A lesson about applying lessons consistently. In an earlier project I lost points because an Excel-for-web export silently dropped my pivot tables — so I adopted a rule: reopen and verify every export before submitting. On the capstone I applied that rule to my dashboard exports and they scored 100%. But I didn't re-apply it to the Excel workbook, and two of its four charts didn't survive the export — five points gone to the exact failure mode I'd already documented. The real takeaway isn't "verify exports"; it's that a QA rule only works when it's applied to *every* deliverable, every time — which is why it now lives as a written checklist gate, not a memory.
